Framework Introduction  ·  Faculty Guide
Why the BE Framework?

Artificial intelligence is changing how students learn, solve problems, and prepare for their future careers. New tools appear rapidly, bringing both opportunities and new questions. As faculty, we do not need to have every answer about AI—but we do have an important role in helping students develop the judgment they will need to use these tools thoughtfully and responsibly.

The BE Framework was created with that goal in mind.

Rather than focusing on specific AI tools or rapidly changing technologies, the framework focuses on five human behaviors that students can develop over time. These behaviors help students make informed decisions, act with integrity, think critically, take responsibility for their choices, and reflect on how AI influences their learning.

These habits will remain valuable long after today’s AI tools have changed.

This playbook is a faculty coaching resource—not a policy document, mandate, or grading system.

The Five BE Behaviors

The BE Framework places the student—not the tool—at the center. Each behavior gives students a practical question they can use to guide their decisions about AI.

  • BE Safe: Do I understand this tool well enough to make smart decisions about how I use it and what I share?
  • BE Honest: Am I accurately representing my own work and my use of AI?
  • BE Critical: Have I evaluated this information before trusting or using it?
  • BE Responsible: Am I making an intentional choice and taking ownership of the outcome?
  • BE Reflective: How is AI affecting my thinking, learning, and professional growth?

Together, these behaviors provide a common language for discussing AI literacy across disciplines. Whether students are writing essays, troubleshooting engines, caring for patients, analyzing data, or developing business plans, the underlying habits of thoughtful AI use remain remarkably consistent.

Learning Over Time

The BE Framework describes a developmental journey—not a checklist. Students are not expected to master these behaviors in a single assignment, course, or semester. They develop them through repeated practice, reflection, feedback, and opportunities to apply their judgment in different contexts.

Introduction

Building Awareness

Students are introduced to the BE behaviors by name and begin recognizing where AI-related decisions appear in their learning. Awareness is the goal. Consistent or independent practice is not yet expected.

Exploration

Practicing Across Courses

Students practice the behaviors in different course contexts with guidance, feedback, and opportunities to reflect. Their application may still be inconsistent—and that inconsistency is a normal part of development.

Application

Program & Workplace Contexts

Students apply the behaviors more independently and consistently. They can explain their reasoning and transfer their judgment to new tools, unfamiliar situations, and discipline-specific professional contexts.

Progression is developmental, not linear. Students may move fluidly between stages depending on the task, tool, stakes, or course context. The goal is not flawless AI use—it is increasingly thoughtful decision-making.
A Framework for Faculty
  • You do not need to become an AI expert before helping students develop better AI judgment.
  • You do not need to introduce all five behaviors at once or add an entirely new assignment.
  • You can begin with the behavior that connects most naturally to your course, assignment, or current instructional challenge.
  • The framework can support student reflection and faculty coaching without becoming a formal grading instrument.
  • Faculty retain responsibility for establishing the expectations for AI use within their own courses and assignments.
You May Already Be Teaching AI Literacy
  • Every time you ask students to evaluate evidence or verify a claim, you are supporting BE Critical.
  • Every time you ask students to document sources or accurately represent their own work, you are supporting BE Honest.
  • Every time students consider the consequences of a professional decision, you are supporting BE Responsible.
  • Every time students examine their learning process or explain how they reached a decision, you are supporting BE Reflective.
  • Every time students consider privacy, limitations, or appropriate tool use, you are supporting BE Safe.
About the Framework
The BE Framework was developed by the OCTC AI Workgroup as a student-centered behavioral interpretation of the Digital Education Council AI Literacy Framework. It translates broad AI literacy competencies into observable habits that can be developed through teaching, practice, feedback, and reflection across disciplines. Additional information about the DEC framework and its relationship to the BE Framework is included in Appendix A.
This guide supports the OCTC AI Literacy Framework, a student-centered translation of the Digital Education Council (DEC) AI Literacy Framework.
