Developing Professional Judgment  ·  Student AI Literacy
Purpose

AI tools are increasingly present in academic, professional, and personal life — but confidence with a tool is not the same as understanding it. BE Safe is about closing that gap.

Students who are safe AI users know enough about how AI works to make deliberate decisions: about what they share, about when to trust output, and about why a confident-sounding response isn't necessarily a correct one. This behavior establishes the foundational awareness that makes every other BE behavior possible.

Students develop this capability through repeated opportunities to engage, reflect, and make increasingly informed decisions over time.

This is a coaching resource — not a grading rubric unless intentionally connected to a course learning outcome.

Developmental Progression

Introduction

FYE 100

Students are introduced to the idea that AI tools have underlying mechanics worth understanding. They may not yet have examined what AI actually is or how it generates responses. The goal here is awareness — establishing that AI is a system with limitations, not an oracle.

Exploration

Across First-Year Courses

Students begin developing a working mental model of how AI generates output. They recognize that AI can be wrong and are starting to think about what that means before acting on its output. They are also becoming more conscious about what they share with AI tools, even if that judgment is not yet consistent.

Application

Program & Workplace Contexts

Students understand how AI works well enough to make deliberate decisions every time — not just when prompted. They know why AI gets things wrong, why what they share matters, and why confident-sounding output is not the same as accurate output. That understanding is operational: it shapes behavior in new and unfamiliar contexts.

Progression is developmental, not linear. Students may move fluidly between stages depending on the task, the tool, or the course context.
I Can Statements
  • I can describe in my own words how AI generates a response — well enough to explain why it might be wrong.
  • I can make thoughtful decisions about what personal, academic, or professional information I share with an AI tool.
  • I can recognize the difference between AI output that sounds confident and output that is actually accurate or reliable.
What This Looks Like · Habits in Practice
  • Pauses before acting on AI output rather than treating it as a final answer
  • Can articulate at least one reason why AI might produce inaccurate or misleading information
  • Makes visible choices about what to share with AI tools — and can explain those choices
  • Asks follow-up questions of AI output rather than accepting the first response
  • Notices when AI output sounds plausible but does not match what they already know to be true
Where This Fits Naturally
  • Any assignment where students use AI to generate, summarize, research, or troubleshoot
  • Discussions or activities involving AI-generated content as a starting point
  • Technical or clinical courses where acting on bad information carries real consequences
  • Research and information literacy activities across all disciplines
  • Orientation and first-year experience contexts where AI norms are being established
  • Reflection activities that ask students to describe how they used a tool and why
Blackboard Ultra Quick Wins
These are examples — not requirements. Choose the ideas that fit naturally into your course, assignment, and students' level of experience.

Discussion

Post a brief AI-generated response on a course-relevant topic and ask students to discuss whether they would trust it — and why or why not.
Sample Prompt
"Here's what an AI said about [course topic]. What would you want to verify before using this? What concerns you, if anything?"

Journal

Ask students to document a moment when they used or considered using an AI tool and reflect on what they knew — or didn't know — about how it worked.
Sample Prompt
"Describe a recent time you used an AI tool. What did you assume it could do? What do you know now that you didn't know then?"

Video Studio

Have students record a short explainer describing how AI generates a response — as if explaining it to someone who has never used it before.
Sample Prompt
"Record a 60–90 second video explaining how an AI tool generates a response. Don't look it up — use what you already understand."

Assignment

Give students a set of AI-generated responses to the same prompt and ask them to rank by trustworthiness — with written justification.
Sample Prompt
"Here are three AI responses to the same question. Rank them from most to least trustworthy and explain your reasoning for each."

AI Chat Conversation

Set up a structured AI conversation where students ask the AI to explain its own limitations — then reflect on whether that explanation itself is trustworthy.
Sample Prompt
"Ask the AI: 'What kinds of questions are you most likely to get wrong, and why?' Then write two sentences about whether you believe its answer."

Knowledge Check

Use low-stakes scenario questions to assess whether students can identify situations where AI use would require extra caution — not right or wrong, but reasoned choices.
Sample Prompt
"A classmate uses AI to summarize a medical article and submits it as research. What are two things they should have done before trusting the AI's version?"
Supporting Student Growth
  • BE Safe is not a technology lesson — it is a judgment lesson. The goal is not for students to understand AI architectures; it is for them to know enough to make smart choices.
  • Avoid framing this as "AI is dangerous." The goal is calibration, not fear. Students who are overly skeptical will not use tools well either.
  • The most revealing question you can ask is "why did you trust that?" — not "is this right?" Build that into your feedback habits.
  • In technical and health sciences courses, connect BE Safe to the professional consequences of trusting unverified output in the field.
  • You do not need to be an AI expert to teach this. Modeling your own uncertainty — "I'm not sure how the tool handles this; let's find out together" — is itself a powerful demonstration of the behavior.
  • Resist the impulse to turn this into AI policing. The goal is to build students' internal judgment, not to catch them misusing tools. Coaching language opens that conversation; punitive language closes it.
Student Reflection Prompts
  1. What do you actually know about how AI generates a response? Where does that knowledge come from — and how confident are you in it?
  2. Have you ever acted on AI output without questioning it? What happened — or what could have happened?
  3. What kinds of information would you be comfortable sharing with an AI tool? What would you hold back? Why?
  4. If AI output sounds completely confident and completely wrong at the same time, how would you know?
  5. How has your sense of what AI can and cannot do changed since you started using it?
